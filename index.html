Black Holes

What is a Black Hole?

A black hole is a place in space where gravity pulls so much that even light can not get out.
The gravity is so strong because matter has been squeezed into a tiny space. This can happen
when a star (3 or more times bigger than our Sun) is dying. Black holes are made of matter
packed so tightly that gravity overwhelms all other forces.

How does a Black hole form?

Most black holes form from the remnants of a large star that dies in a supernova explosion.
(Smaller stars become dense neutron stars, which are not massive enough to trap light.)
If the total mass of the star is large enough (about three times the mass of the Sun),
it can be proven theoretically that no force can keep the star from collapsing under the influence of gravity.

How big can Black Holes be?

A black hole can be bigger than our solar system or tiny as an atom. Stellar-mass black holes are
typically in the range of 10 to 100 solar masses, while the supermassive black holes at the centers of galaxies
can be millions or even billions of solar masses.The supermassive black hole at the center of the Milky Way,
Sagittarius A*, is 4.3 million solar masses.

How does a Black Hole lose mass?

Hawking Radiation - Quantum particles that appear near the event horizon and are able to escape it,
stealing the Black Hole's mass away. Over eons the radiation gets stronger and the Black Hole evaporates
until it disappears in a flash.
